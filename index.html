How it all began.

The History of Sampling is long and winding. Here are some highlights over time.

The Earliest Sampling.

Using another artist's music in your own is not a new concept. Western Classical Composers have been writing Theme and Variations and Fantasias for centuries, and Jazz Musicians have long performed and improvised off of another performer's well-known melody . One of the most cherished and respected pieces for Classical Guitar is a Theme and Variation off of a Mozart tune, the theme from the Magic Flute. The Magic Flute Theme is Mozart’s, but the final arrangement and variations are written by Fernando Sor decades later.

Early 20th Century

It wasn't until much later that an artist could take the exact sound of another performance and use it in their compositions. That capability was first possible in the 1920's with Edison’s phonograph, and this analog sampling technique using discs, called Musique Concrete, were used with much effort by acts like the Beatles

Late 20th Century

Sampling in music really only took off with the invention of the commercial electronic sampler in 1971. And by the mid-1980’s, most major keyboard manufacturers had some form of sampler for sale. The invention of the electronic sampler collided with the birth of hip-hop, where it soon found a home in the Bronx. Here, Carribean immigrants would 'Toast' over loops of b-sides on Disco LPs by manipulating actual LP discs. The toast, or the act of speaking over a disco beat, was done to add more rhythm to a dance track, extending the length of a song and allowing for more dance time. This quickly evolved into rapping, with DJ's creating musical backdrops primarily through samples of existing music.

21st Century

Nowadays, sampling provides the backbone for much of pop music. In fact, 20 of the top 50 songs on the 2018 pop charts all had some form of sample. From “Nice For What” to “Lucid Dreams”, samples permeate much of the music people listen to, whether they realize it or not

Why It Matters.

It's a Small World

  Modern Culture is not made in a vacuum. As it was so eloquently put in the the Current Affair’s article “The Question of Cultural Appropriation”, “We wear textiles from central Asia; we eat “Italian” pasta brought to Europe from China in the 13th century; and we developed our earliest legal framework from East Africans. (And that “Italian” violin is a Persian derivative).” The banjo was designed from an African instrument, and now is the most iconic sound of the predominantly white Country music genre.

  Pop music has a tendency to reflect this cultural landscape. During the conservative 1950’s, Elvis “Hound Dog” captured the ears and hearts of Middle America and catapulted him into fame, despite being a sanitized cover of Big Mama Thornton’s original of the same name. It far outsold the original almost entirely due to the color of his skin, which made him a far more marketable artist than Big Mama Thornton. Ragtime and Jazz was once considered the music of African Americans and lower class America, while Chopin and other late romantic composers of the early 1900’s were the music of the upper class (rich and white).

  These stark divides that exist in our society nowadays means that any thoughtful way to bridge the divide between race, culture, and decade make pop music a powerful tool in bring people together. Big Freedia can proudly flaunt her LGBTQ status and Cardi B can celebrate Latin American heritage in times where the President of the United States is attacking immigrants and denying rights to transgender children. The storytelling power of sampling allows music to become more expressive, reach a larger audience, and put important people in front of an audience they have never had before. And in a time where people are quick to draw divisions, we need more ways to connect ourselves together. Pop music has never been so powerful, meaningful, and diverse. And it has only been helped by sampling.

  Sampling, as shown above, has given artists the tools to be powerful and diverse in ways artists before have only dreamed of. Sampling has completely revolutionized the art of musical production. As said by Josh Kun, "You have to have a relationship with the archives... you gotta know your sources. you gotta know your history. Crossfading involves historical conciousness. you must know the past to invent the future." It has connected artists from different generations, given listeners experiences with music from halfway across the globe, and even allowed for layered storytelling. This is the power of sampling.
