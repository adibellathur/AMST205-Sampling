Toxic - Britney Spears


  As the popularity of hip-hop grew, so did the process of sampling: by the early 2000's, some of the biggest pop (not just rap) hits were heavily using samples. The range of sources has increased as well, as it is no longer the past music of the Bronx, but more international sounds. Hip Hop producers such as Timbaland and Just Blaze started sampling Bollywood songs, adding international flare to their songs. Jay Z and Kanye West rapped on top of Raje Shwari's vocals in 'The Bounce', and Eric Sermon and Redman traded bars on top of the 'Chandi Ka Badan' sample Just Blaze used to produce 'React'. All of the biggest The formula became so successful artists started to craft original, unsampled works using this Indian style of music. Timbaland and Missy Elliott specifically learned Bhangra in order to produce their hit song "Get Ur Freak On". Scott Storch, one of the most successful producers of the late 90's and early 2000's, was the trailblazer for using Indian and Middle Eastern sounds in his biggest hits. He said in a 2006 interview that “I have great respect for that type of music… That way of playing is different than our American music. The notes are different, the scales are different. They have half-notes that are in between the tones. It’s some really interesting stuff.”

  The success of Indian sounds in Hip-hop started to spread quickly into the pop charts. Female Pop Artists in particular became the champions of the sounds, heavily sampling Eastern sounds. The most famous of these is Britney Spears' 'Toxic'. Sampling 'Tere Mere Beech Mein' by Lata Mangeshkar and S. P. Balasubrahmanyam from the 1981 film Ek Duuje Ke Liye, producers Bloodshy and Avant gave Spears a new canvas to craft an entirely new identity for herself, away from the Teen Pop she had become the posterchild for, towards a more mature sound. Her footsteps were quickly followed by Beyonce, who's second single for her debut album used Scott Storch to surround herslef in in the same middle eastern sounds that were so popular at the time. This tactic was later also used by Kelly Rowland (Work) and the Black Eyed Peas (Don't Phunk With My Heart).
